My Motivation

My wife is an educator. In their academia world they focus a lot on filling in the "gaps." For lack of a better term, I too have found my personality leaning in this gap-filling role direction. In school, gaps refer to those empty crevacies that were over-looked or never quite structurally sound in the mind and curriculum being taught to children. In my life I like to see gaps as opportunities. I enjoy the thrill of pursuing the problem solving process. It usually begins with a person, sometimes frustrated and aware and other times simply wishing/needing something they cannot make happen. From the dialogue during an initial needs-assesment to the construction and implementation of a plan, I love gap-filling. But from my paper resume - you'd never know this was the role I routinely filled in the organizations I have served. In addition to filling this role for my co-workers and volunteers, I've been fueled by this opportunity as a mentor to several and have even been a part of two small-buisness start-ups as a result of my passion for the gaps. There is a sense of pride in filling a need that motivates me to be a top performer.

My Value Proposition

My resume speaks to many of the technical skills that I have developed over the past decade, and while that is important for completing daily tasks; I believe I have a very unique value due to my vast experience in leading and developing volunteers teams. I have been leading and developing teams of volunteers for over 8 years. All of the volunteer positions have weekly responsibilities to show up and be prepared to play crucial roles each week. The pressure of every 7 days and 52 weeks per year to have competent and quality volunteers, will make or break a person’s ability to lead. That’s exactly where I have come from and have been highly successful in recruiting, developing, and maintaining teams of volunteers that are excellent in their roles. I would argue that leading and recruiting volunteers to fill positions that are required for the organizations success is a combination of art and science. Nothing teaches leadership better than the pressure of depending on a group of volunteers to show up and preform. Leading volunteers can be tricky, they are both the client I serve and are fulfilling a role or job that I am the manager of. Simply put, they are both the client and the employee, so early on in my work as a volunteer manager, I had to learn how to navigate the delicate role of holding someone to a high standard of excellence while serving them well as the client. I believe the value of leading these types comes in the moments when the need arises to interface with the client, communicate to the Gartner team as well as the client, and problem solve with both the client and Gartner team at the same time. Leading volunteer teams requires a strategic approach to communication and a clear understanding of what is being asked of them. I will bring to the table a decade of communicating both verbal and written, as well as, a developed strategy for communicating vision, purpose, and values.
